Artist Bio

Vanessa Aye Sabai Myint / VANZ (born in 2000, Sydney; Blacktown; based in Melbourne and Singapore) is a multidisciplinary artist working across video, sound, installation, and digital media. Much of her practice unfolds in the digital space, where she treats the screen as both a mirror and a diary‚Äîan intimate site for reflection, play, and storytelling.

VANZ is interested in how digital spaces can hold memory, capture fleeting emotions, and create small portals into inner worlds. Her works often are about the appreciation of digital space while also bring up conversations of digital manifestations into physical manifestations, and how digital spaces have shaped today's youth, culture and psychology, creating poetic environments where audiences are encouraged to pause, listen, and wander.

Vanessa is also engaged in collaborative and curatorial work, most notably as Gallery Executive at STARCH in Singapore, where she has helped shape exhibitions and public programs, including projects for Singapore Art Week. She values the digital not just as a medium, but as a community space, where connection, vulnerability, and creativity can all exist side by side.

üìÅ You Think You Know Me

You Think You Know Me

2025 Cyanotypes / Receipts / Mixed Media

Artist Statement

In you think you know me, I explore the delicate space between anonymity and intimacy, between the self we present and the one we withhold. Using found receipts, fragmented images of the body, and worn denim, the work becomes a meditation on the fleeting relationships we form with strangers and with ourselves. These materials‚Äîfamiliar yet overlooked‚Äîserve as quiet witnesses to our daily rituals, choices, and vulnerabilities.

Receipts, often discarded without thought, act here as emotional artifacts. They mark moments of presence, transactions, routines, and the echoes of lives we may never fully see. Layered together, they form a kind of personal archive that speaks to both connection and distance: the intimacy of recognition without knowing, of shared experience without names.

The fragmented photographs of bodies‚Äîcropped and abstracted‚Äîgesture toward both self-exposure and concealment. They resist the complete gaze, offering glimpses that challenge the viewer to reconsider how we come to know another person, or even ourselves. Who do we become in the eyes of a stranger? What parts of us are misread, assumed, or erased?

Grounded in a feminist perspective, the work critiques how femme bodies are consumed and catalogued‚Äîboth in media and in memory. Denim, marked by wear and repetition, evokes labour, care, and resilience. The hanging, layered textures echo the emotional residues we carry, both visible and hidden.

you think you know me invites the viewer to linger in the tension between closeness and distance, the familiar and the unknown. It is a quiet assertion: that beneath every surface lies complexity, and that true understanding, of others or ourselves, is always partial, always in flux.
Medium
Cyanotypes, receipts, denim, photography
Themes
Identity, assumption, material culture, intimacy
üìÅ A Piece Of Home + A Piece Of You

A Piece Of Home +
A Piece Of You

2025 Brass

Info

A Piece Of Home + A Piece Of You explores nostalgia.

A Piece Of You carries the engravement of the song title "You Are My Sunshine". A song my grandmother used to sing to me, even near her death. Encrypted in binary codes for my love for technology. The code is all jumbled up to mimic the first bedroom I slept and grew up in when I first moved to Singapore and met my brothers. There I used to scribble on the walls, thinking it was a giant canvas. My grandmother taught me how to draw stars, resulting in me filling the 'canvas' with stars.

The star being the piece of home, with engravements of birth dates of family members, including the newest member, which is Thea, my brother's daughter.

The work isn't complete till my memory fades, and I no longer know any birth dates of the immediate family members. Dates will keep engraving as the family tree extends, even if there is no more space for the dates, numbers will be engraved over and over, till I no longer know their dates.

This work is dedicated to my mother and grandmother.
Medium
Brass
Dimensions
10.5 cm
Themes
Nostalgia, family, memory, home
üìÅ Hangry_Consume

Hangry_Consume

2024 Video

Info

Consume, Consume, Consume

In the dim glow of the screen, the meal waits, steam fading like a dying breath. I search for the perfect video to sanctify the moment, and in that brief hesitation the food grows cold, as if recoiling from me. The internet festers beside my plate: endless dramas that aren't dramas, voices clamouring for my attention, influencers whispering into the dark.

Our senses decay softly, almost tenderly. Taste dulls first. Touch follows, then Sight. The more we scroll, the more the world around us rots at the edges. We are not devoured by the internet, we are the ravenous ones, gnawing at its infinite body, greedy for one more clip, one more bite of nothing.
Medium
Video
Themes
Consumption, desire, digital decay

üìÅ First Date

First Date

2024 Video

Info

This work began with a story a friend told me about her first date. She was simply enjoying a slice of cake when the man watching her said, "I wish I was that fork." What he imagined was desire. What she felt was disgust. Something as innocent as eating became sexualised, and something as sweet as cake became tainted. That shift ‚Äî that quiet violation ‚Äî is where this piece lives.

I was interested in how the male gaze can turn a woman into an object without her consent, even in the smallest moments. The work reflects the tension between wanting to feel safe and being seen in ways that warp your sense of self. As the imagery fragments and the body becomes harder to recognise, the piece leans into female rage: soft, controlled, and simmering.

It speaks to the emotional labour of navigating heterosexual relationships, where understanding and anger coexist, and where desire too often becomes entitlement.
Medium
Video
Themes
Male gaze, consent, female rage, objectification
